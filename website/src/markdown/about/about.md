# What is the Socially Responsible Computing Handbook?

The Socially Responsible Computing Handbook (SRCH) is an open resource reference guide to navigating and discussing contemporary and ongoing intersections between technology and society from a variety of academic perspectives. It is an ongoing collaboration between the [Socially Responsible Computing program](https://responsible.cs.brown.edu/) in the Brown University Computer Science Department and the [Center for Technological Responsibility, Reimagination, and Redesign](https://cntr.brown.edu/) (CNTR) in the Brown University Data Science Institute.

SRCH seeks to provide a central resource that compiles the current thinking and scholarship on sociotechnical issues, presenting a roadmap of topics and ideas that can be successfully used in and out of the classroom. SRCH is primarily targeted towards teachers, professors, teaching assistants, and other academics who want to include materials on ethics, society, and computing in their curricula. Each section of SRCH is written to be understood at the undergraduate level without prior knowledge, allowing the Handbook itself to be used as a tool for teaching. In essence, SRCH serves as a form of continuing education for the teachers of computer science, ethics, and society, especially for those who may have less knowledge in one of these areas.

SRCH is not quite a textbook, but rather a [scholarly dynamic reference work](https://plato.stanford.edu/about.html), maintaining a growing set of interconnected articles about society and technology for a broad academic audience. In doing so, SRCH aims to close the gaps between current sociotechnical research, the classroom, and technology development.

# How to Use the Handbook

The SRC Handbook is currently organized into three core modules: [Privacy](/privacy), [Accessibility](/accessibility), and [Automated Decision Making](/automatedDecisionMaking), with a fourth Generative AI module coming soon. Each module contains primers aligned to learning objectives, with side panels throughout that expand on key ideas, definitions, and case studies. Each primer can be understood on its own, although many reference content and explanations from other primers. Use these primers to:

- Learn more about the intersections between technology and society

- Structure your lesson or course with embedded ethical modules

- Give students real examples that connect tech to society

- Foster inclusive, critical thinking in the classroom on critical topics

- Adapt and contribute content so it remains relevant and impactful

# A Living Resource

SRCH is a living resource maintained by faculty, graduate students, and undergraduate students at Brown University. Content is held to both internal and external editorial standards before publication to ensure scholarly rigor. Given the rapidly changing nature of some of the topics covered, we continuously update published materials to be as current and accurate as possible.

As part of this process, the SRCH team relies on a broad team of undergraduate and graduate students to develop and maintain the Handbook. This allows students the opportunity to engage in deep research and receive close mentorship from faculty, supporting broader academic goals. Our students and faculty come from highly diverse academic backgrounds, including Behavioral Decision Sciences, Economics, Philosophy, Mathematics, International and Public Affairs, Public Health, Anthropology, Cybersecurity, Religious Studies, Urban Studies, Computational Biology, Data Science, English, and Computer Science. Most students working on the Handbook at Brown are compensated through an independent study credit or pay.

# History of the Project

:::timeline
[May 2024]

![Michelle L. Ding](/assets/member-photos/michelle.png "wrap-right")

SRCH was founded by Michelle L. Ding (Brown University Class of 2025, Current Ph.D Student), with advisors Julia Netter and Suresh Venkatasubramanian.

As a former Socially Responsible Computing Teaching Assistant (STA), Michelle saw the need for a curated library of resources for STAs to support them in teaching socio-technical topics like privacy, AI governance, accessibility, sustainability, and more. She also saw the need for more opportunities for students to get involved with socio-technical research on campus, receive mentorship from interdisciplinary faculty, and grow in community with like-minded students. The result is the SRC Handbook: a project that aims to improve the quality of socially computing education taught in Brown University's Computer Science courses by building a dedicated pipeline from current sociotechnical research to the CS classroom.

[Fall 2024]
Michelle, Julia, and Suresh recruited the first cohort of students into the project for the privacy and product teams. The privacy team developed the first set of primers and the product team developed a prototype and conducted user studies with STAs.

[Spring 2025]
SRCH added AI and accessibility teams to cover additional topics, while the first primers were published later that semester.

[Today*]
Julia and Suresh continue to advise the project along with Meredith Mendola, program manager of the Center for Tech Responsibility.
:::
