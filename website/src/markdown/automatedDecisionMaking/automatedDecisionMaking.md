Artificial intelligence now shapes how institutions sort, predict, recommend, classify, and decide. It helps determine who is flagged as risky, which applications receive extra scrutiny, what content people see, and how knowledge, creativity, and labor are increasingly mediated by machines. For computer science students, this makes AI not just a technical field, but also a social one. To study or build AI today is to confront questions about power, fairness, responsibility, and public impact. This section introduces core ethical and social questions raised by AI, especially in systems that influence people’s opportunities, treatment, and visibility. Across primers, modules, and teaching resources, the goal is not to treat ethics as an add-on, but as part of what it means to understand AI well.

This section focuses on automated decision-making systems used to support or make judgments about people, often in contexts such as hiring, lending, education, healthcare, policing, or public benefits. The central concerns here include bias, fairness, explainability, legitimacy, and whether a system should be used at all. Separately, generative AI produces new content such as text, images, audio, or code and raises major concerns include authorship, copyright, likeness, misinformation, deskilling, synthetic media, and the limits of controlling outputs. Both matter, but they call for different vocabularies, examples, and governing strategies.

### Why This Section Exists

AI systems do not simply reflect the world. They represent it through categories, variables, labels, objectives, and thresholds chosen by people. Those choices are always partial. They highlight some features of reality and suppress others. They encode judgments about what matters, what can be measured, what counts as success, and whose perspective is treated as standard.

That is why this section begins from a basic premise: technical systems are never only technical. Data collection reflects social assumptions. Models inherit constraints from datasets and design goals. Fairness metrics formalize particular understandings of equity while leaving others out. Governance frameworks decide which actors are accountable, when intervention is required, and what risks institutions are willing to tolerate. This section assumes that readers do not need advanced statistics or machine learning expertise to begin engaging with these questions. A general understanding of how data and models are used is enough to start. What matters most is a willingness to ask what assumptions are being made, whose interests are being served, and what consequences follow from technical design choices.

### Recommended Way to Read This Section

The topics we cover are meant to be read together, not in isolation: Justice asks what fairness, equality, legitimacy, and accountability might mean before those ideas are translated into technical systems. Sources of Bias uncover how distortions and inequities emerge throughout the AI pipeline, from data collection and labeling to modeling choices and deployment contexts. Fairness shows efforts to formalize competing goals and why no single metric can resolve every normative tension. Governance draws the previous themes together and asks how institutions should assign responsibility, create oversight, and respond to the real-world harms or limitations of AI systems.

### Key Definitions

The definitions below are intentionally practical. Following the Aspen Digital framing of [Lineage Definitions](https://www.aspendigital.org/report/defining-technologies-of-our-time-ai/), we treat definitions of AI not as timeless truths but as context-dependent choices shaped by purpose, scope, and use.

* Artificial Intelligence (AI): Any artificial system that performs tasks under varying and unpredictable circumstances without significant human oversight, or that can learn from experience and improve performance when exposed to data sets. Possible other definitions are outlined [here](https://www.aspendigital.org/report/defining-tech-lineage-ai/) or if otherwise specified in the primer.   
* [Automated Decision-Making System](https://www.aspendigital.org/report/defining-tech-lineage-ads/) (ADS): A computational process, including one derived from machine learning, statistics, or other data processing or artificial intelligence techniques, that makes a decision or facilitates human decision making, that impacts consumers.  
* [AI system](https://www.aspendigital.org/report/defining-tech-lineage-ai-system/): A machine-based system that can, for a given set of human-defined objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments. AI systems are designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy.

### A Note on Definition

One of the core lessons of AI governance is that definitions do political and practical work. They determine what falls in scope, which harms are made visible, who is responsible, and what kinds of oversight become possible. That is why this section does not treat definitions as fixed. Instead, it encourages readers to ask what a definition includes, what it leaves out, and what becomes easier or harder to see once a particular term is chosen.
