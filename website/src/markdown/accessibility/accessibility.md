### What Is Accessibility?

As technology becomes more deeply embedded in how people work, learn, communicate, and access services, questions of who can actually use that technology have become increasingly urgent. Accessibility is not a fixed standard. It shifts over time as users’ needs change and as laws, design practices, and social debates evolve. What counts as accessible, who gets to define it, and what obligations designers and organizations have are all questions that remain open and contested.

While accessibility benefits all users, it is often deprioritized and treated as an afterthought in the design process. The Accessibility section provides readers with a holistic overview of the importance of Accessibility, focusing particularly on digital accessibility. This section also identifies factors that cause people, specifically designers, to subconsciously and consciously ignore Accessibility.

This section introduces accessibility through five primers: [What is Accessibility](accessibility/whatIsAccessibility), Accessibility Standards (coming soon!), [Intersections with Other Values](accessibility/intersectionsWithOtherValues), [Biases in Accessibility](accessibility/biasesInDesign), and [Design Processes](accessibility/designProcesses). Each stands on its own, but we recommend reading them in order. The primers move from foundational definitions to practical design methods. Along the way, they also cover the standards, values, and biases that shape accessibility.

The overall goal of these primers is to give readers the tools and awareness they need to think more critically about accessibility and make more informed, inclusive design decisions.

### Key Definitions

- Accessibility: a fundamental concept that ensures all individuals have equal access to information and technology
- Cognitive Bias: systematic patterns in how we think that can lead us to overlook certain users' needs or make assumptions about how people interact with systems
- Mental Model: internal representations of how individuals expect something to function based on past experiences
- Judgmental Heuristics: mental shortcuts that help people make quick decisions by drawing on what has worked before
- Usability: the ease of access and use of a product or service
