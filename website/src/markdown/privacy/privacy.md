### Why Privacy?

We live in a new age for privacy. The advent of digital sensors and other technologies allow us to collect increasing amounts and new types of data about each other. The internet has introduced a new form of public social connection, bringing the traditional town square to a place that is now easily monitored and permanently recorded. Breakthroughs in statistical methods have made it easier to identify and connect people from limited pieces of information and to analyze monumental amounts of data about both individuals and groups. When we don’t have privacy, how we are monitored, what is being monitored, and who is monitoring us have all dramatically changed since the beginning of the 20th century. All the while, companies and individuals can now directly benefit from these potential losses in privacy by collecting mass amounts of data, creating a new type of economy that has spawned some of the most wealthy organizations and individuals in human history.  
   
These changes have forced us to fully rethink privacy as a concept, further complicating a topic that has been open to debate and confusion for multiple centuries. Privacy is often something that we believe we have and can lose, with many perspectives holding that we might have a right to privacy in our day to day lives. This leaves open many critical questions, including where privacy is needed, what things might need to be private, and whether we even want privacy at all, among others. Especially in a digital age, we’re forced to consider whether our past ideas about privacy are even applicable to describing the current world, or if we need to try to create new models of privacy that better describe what might be going on.

### How Does Everything Connect?

Much like the other parts of this handbook, each of the sections on privacy don’t assume that you have read any of the others, nor do they directly build on each other in any way. Keeping that in mind, reading each of the sections provides a rough overview of data, privacy, and the role of technology in shaping these ideas.

We recommend that readers begin with either _What Is Privacy?_ or _Extent of Data Collection_, which will provide multiple definitions for what privacy is, what data might be, and why more data is collected now than before. For a more thorough view of data, readers should move to _Data Ownership and Surveillance Capitalism_. For more on the theory of privacy, _Value of Privacy_ discusses reasons why we might want privacy, while _Is Privacy Bad?_ explores potential tradeoffs against privacy. Readers may then look to see how privacy currently exists in practice, ongoing threats to privacy, and potential solutions to these problems, which are covered in _Surveillance_, _Consent_, _Re-identification and Differential Privacy_, and _Encryption and Otherwise_. _Privacy Regulations_ then summarizes the current legal landscape for privacy and the underlying principles guiding regulations.

### What Do We Assume?

Privacy, across the technical and non-technical fields that discuss it, is a highly fraught concept with competing and unclear definitions. Much of the debate covering the basic ideas about privacy is not settled and will likely need to continue as technology advances. Despite this, we take a handful of stances on privacy that make writing about it more coherent at an undergraduate level. These include:

- Privacy is likely a right that all people should have.
- Privacy is a coherent, separate right and not a group of overlapping, pre-existing rights.
- Privacy can be held by both individuals and groups.
- Privacy is generally something that most people want to have in most cases.
- Something is lost or there is some harm when we don’t have privacy.
- The amount of privacy someone has can vary depending on context.

Each of these statements have been disputed in the literature. While these debates are both lively and important, we find they are often not useful for introducing ideas about privacy and data or to explore the intersection between privacy and technology at an undergraduate, non-philosophical level. Our view roughly aligns with the broad colloquial understanding of privacy, which allows us to explore more topics and better meet students where they are at.
